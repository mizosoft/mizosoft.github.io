![Utopia](/assets/images/utopia.png)

Morality is what fundamentally governs human action. After all, there is an effectively infinite number of ways to act in any given circumstance, with the ensuing consequences that emerge through a complex interplay in a huge network of causalities and other actions by other agents. The most fundamental human need is to know what actions are more worthy of pursuit than others, or what one *ought* to do. So, what one ought to do? A consequentialist would say that one ought to do what has good consequences, and a utilitarianist would continue, in order to not halt at an answer that simply delays the question, that what has good consequences is what maximizes the welfare of [human] beings. A deontologist, however, would contend that one ought to do what's right, simply because one is obliged to, with no or little regard to consequences. As to whether what's right emerges from religion, culture, law, reason, intuition, or a conglomerate of all, is certainly a matter of disagreement. The dichotomy between duty and consequence runs deep through moral philosophy. Plato, however, gives us a third answer.

## Justice

In Republic, a Socratic dialogue that is more or less a lecture, although one that is not without occasional bursts of objections, teasing, and back-and-forth that are particularly thrilling, our Platonic Socrates aims to answer two questions: What is justice? And, why be just? Justice, to the ancient Greeks, meant more than mere fairness or law enforcement. It signified a deeper, ethical meaning that is more akin to *goodness* or *morality*.

Plato presents a challenge: defining justice merely in terms of actions runs the risk of only valuing it for its semblance. It is then to seem moral that is desirable, not to be so. Thus, Plato argues that justice is less about what to do and more about what to be. It is a particular psychological disposition. To find the essence of such disposition, Plato doesn't tackle the subject of the question, that is, the individual, directly. Through a political analogy, Plato looks into where it is easier to find the nature and influence of justice, namely, a city-state that comprises many individuals. He erects such a state from the ground up and searches into how justice manifests itself, and what effect it would have. After all, politics is more manageable than psychology, and there is a mutual causality between the two; certain kinds of individuals produce certain kinds of governments, and vice versa. 

The construction of the state, which is in fact an attempt at Utopia, is rather amusing to follow. That is partly due to its unorthodox ideas mixed with undeniably positive aspects. The state is to comprise three classes: the craftsmen (carpenters, blacksmiths, farmers, etc.), a military class (referred to as auxiliaries), and a ruling class (referred to as guardians). The ruling class is to be formed of philosophers, as they, and only they, are capable of acquiring true knowledge and wisdom, from which they can derive the path to an ideal state. Guardians and auxiliaries are to live a communal life, with everything from dwellings to wives and children being shared among them, thus proposing some form of class-specific communism[^1]. The third class, or the commoners, are allowed to own, but are made sure to never own too much. Women, although admitted to be physically weaker, are given equal opportunities to men in regards to protecting and ruling the state, thus suggesting some form of gender-wise meritocracy. Society is to be rigidly stratified. Each class minds only its occupation towards perfection and never dabbles with the other's. Occupations rely on inherent ability. Children, who are upbrought by the state, are to be conceived with selective breeding, so the higher classes beget the like. Art and innovation are censored lest they cause decadence and anarchy. Children are carefully educated in gymnasium, music, arithmetic, planar and solid geometry, planetary mechanics, and dialectic. Propaganda is to be justified, even encouraged, in as much as it is beneficial to the maintenance and improvement of the state. What we have here is an elitist authoritarianism that's run by philosophers. However, it differs from other authoritarianisms in that the philosophers rule purely out of obligation, not self-interest. Their goal is to maximize the overall happiness of the citizens rather than dominate them. In Plato's state, power is a means, not an end.

## The Multitudinous Self

The three political classes represent psychological counterparts. Guardians reflect reason. Auxiliaries reflect spirit, or the will to apply reason. Craftsmen represent the multitude of appetitive and conflicting desires in an individual, which nevertheless need to be cared for. Much like the state, the individual is wise in as much as he is ruled by reason. She is brave in as much as she has the will to apply such reason. He is temperate in as much as there is a mutual agreement between each of these forces on who gets to rule. And finally, she is just in as much as each force is only concerned with its role, never attempting to exercise what it never had the aptitude for. All much like a harmonious piece of music, with all its distinct notes seamlessly and nicely blending together. Thus, justice is to rule the multitude of desires only by reason, and only through spirit. Here, we can see that the self, or the soul, as Plato would put it, is a multiplicity looking for harmony, rather than a united whole. Why be just, then? Well, one can hardly dispute the claim that psychological harmony leads to satisfaction and inner peace, which are desirable both for their own sake and for their consequences.

## The True World

We cannot mention Republic without [briefly] mentioning Plato's Theory of Forms — When you look around, what do you see? Chances are you see objects that you can touch, smell, hear, and, perhaps unsurprisingly, see. That is what we can aptly call the world of sensible objects. But do you remember the last time you've solved a math problem, or carried out a similarly abstract endeavor? Didn't it feel like a disconnect from such world? The manner you manipulate abstract concepts, that are not perfectly realizable in the sense world[^2], is rather peculiar. It is this dichotomy that, perhaps partly, urged Plato to suggest a different world, namely the world of ideas (forms). The world of ideas is the true world, and the world of objects is merely an imitation of the former[^3]. The world of objects is ephemeral, and the world of ideas is eternal. What's eternal must be real, and what's only ephemeral must be illusive[^4]. Only through reason can one assimilate true knowledge in the world of ideas, rather than have mere opinions in illusive the world of objects. Good, itself, is an idea. You can only become truly good once you know what good really is, and that can only be done with reason. Plato's morality is one of rationality.

However, does mathematical training necessarily make one more virtuous? It can certainly help one practice virtue more effectively, but can rationality derive virtue itself? Can an *is* truly derive an *ought*? After all, it was decided that Plato's philosopher kings *ought* to maximize the happiness of the state *before* it is known that they best use reason to do so.

[^1]: It is seen that the military people ought not to own private property, lest greed divert them from their true purpose, which is to protect the state rather than to possess it. I assume that military juntas would be automatically adversarial to Plato.

[^2]: Can you have an iron stick whose length is a multiple of &#960;? You can try by first constructing a base stick, starting at 3 of any unit, say meters. Then add 0.1 meters, then 0.04, then 0.001, then 0.0005. You can never run out of fractions. However, you'd reach a length that is impossible to be independently added, particularly about 2 * 10^-9 meters, which is the diameter of an iron atom. Thus no iron stick whose length is a multiple of &#960;, and hence can be a circle's circumference, can ever exist. A true circle doesn't exist except as an idea.

[^3]: As explained by Plato's famous allegory of the cave; imagine yourself being trapped in a cave, only being able to perceive mere shadows that are cast by a far light source from outside. The shadows belong to, not even actual objects, but only statues of such, thus indicating we are twice removed from reality. Interestingly, this allegory is what, perhaps partly, inspired The Matrix movies.
